# Framework Messaging Design

## Context

Deeproot is built on an underlying framework for organising life around hard, meaningful pursuits. The framework currently has no standalone explanation — the app's welcome overlay jumps straight to "here's how the tool works" without naming the method or articulating the thinking behind it.

The goal is to communicate Constraint Cascading as a framework that exists independently of the tool.

## Naming

- **Constraint Cascading** = the framework/methodology (the idea)
- **Deeproot** = the tool that implements it (the product)

The framework should be usable with a napkin. The tool is optional. This separation gives the framework intellectual credibility with the target audience and lets the tool convert people who already buy the method.

## Audience

People already familiar with productivity frameworks (GTD, Essentialism, OKRs, Wardley Mapping, Theory of Constraints). They appreciate a new lens and will evaluate it against what they already know.

## Approach

**"The Failed Session"** — lead with a specific moment of wanting to fly (paragliding/skydiving) but being unable to. Use that failure to reverse-engineer the first cascade, then zoom out into the framework, then end with practical advice for building your own.

## Tone & Voice

- First person throughout. Specific over abstract.
- No selling — Deeproot gets one sentence at the end.
- Acknowledge the lineage honestly. The audience knows Wardley, TOC, etc.
- End with a tool (the five principles), not a manifesto.

## Deliverables

### A. Standalone Essay (Primary)

~1000-1500 words. Long-form piece written in Alex's voice. Lives as its own page — channel TBD (could be a route within Deeproot like `/about` or `/framework`, or a separate personal site page).

#### Outline

**Working title:** "Constraint Cascading: Organising Life Around Hard Things"

**1. The Failed Session** (~200 words)
- A specific day you couldn't fly. What happened, what it felt like.
- The frustration of wanting to do the hard thing and watching the day slip.
- End with: "I started asking why."

**2. The First Cascade** (~300 words)
- Walk backward from the failure: "I couldn't fly because ___."
- Each "because" reveals the next dependency. Go 3-4 layers deep.
- The moment it clicks: this isn't one problem, it's a chain.
- Name it here: "I started calling this constraint cascading."

**3. The Framework** (~300 words)
- **Hard things at the top.** The goal that scares you is the organising principle, not something you fit around the edges.
- **Recursive constraint mapping.** "What does this depend on?" until you hit bedrock — something you can act on today.
- **The permission to prune.** If it's not in the hierarchy, it's not a priority. Not forever — just not now.

**4. The Lineage** (~150 words)
- Quick nods: Wardley Mapping (dependency chains), Theory of Constraints (bottleneck focus), HTA (decomposition), Essentialism (the filter).
- What's different: combining "hard things" as the top-level value with recursive mapping and a clear deprioritisation mechanism.

**5. How to Build Your Own** (~250 words)
- Don't start from scratch. Start from failures.
- Every time you wanted to do the thing but couldn't — that's a validated dependency.
- Sweep categories: physical, financial, temporal, relational, logistical.
- First version is always incomplete. Refine every time reality bumps against the model.
- One line: "I built Deeproot to make this visual." Link.
